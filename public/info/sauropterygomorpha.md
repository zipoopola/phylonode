Sauropterygians, Ichtyosaurs, and Thalattosaurs placement in the reptile tree has also been complex. They have been grouped separaetly and together multiple times within Pantestudines, or even basal to the entirity of Reptilia for Ichthys.

Other theories propose Sauropterygins to be closely related to Turtles, due to basal members of the group having armoured bodies, but This cladogram follows the work of Simões et al (2022) and Wolniewicz et al (2023). These are morphological studies with complex Baysian algorithms used to sort the tree.

Notably they both recover the `Marine Reptile Complex' within Archosauromorpha rather than grouped with Turtles. An intruiging piece of evidence that supports this placement is the discovery of a well-preserved Prosaurosphargis, a very basal member closely related to Sauropterygians, which had its own body armour.
![Prosaurosphargis](images/info_images/Prosaurosphargis.jpg)

This creature was very Archosaurian in morphology, in terms of skull (diapsid - no signs of closing as in turtles), spine, and limbs. This provides evidence that basal members of the Sauropterygomorphs group could have developed body armour independently of the Turtles, and that they, in fact, aren't close relatives to Pappochelys (an ancient Testudine). The theory is that armoured bodies may have supported a transition to water, to act as ballasts.
